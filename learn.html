“Teaching science, technology, engineering, or math is easily the best thing any young person can do with their life. If you’re not pursuing teaching, you are pursuing a life of misery, loneliness, and probably a bunch of terrible STDs.”

— Anonymous important and certainly very real person

At the 2011 Power Suit & Clapping Convention—aka The State Of The Union address—President Obama challenged the nation to produce 100,000 excellent new science, technology, engineer, and math (STEM) teachers by the year 2021. Unlike the majority of things promised by elected officials, this particular commitment actually resulted in, you know, something happening.

The reason for this plea: the United States hasn’t exactly been the sharpest bulb in the knife shed when it comes to international rankings in math and science. Which means we either start putting more great teachers into our nation’s classrooms or we invent some entirely new rankings system. But even that would require a crap ton of math, so the teaching option seems like our best bet.

Since Obama issued his 2011 challenge, the organization 100Kin10 has rallied hundreds of companies, advocates, non-profits, and teacher-prep programs around a single shared mission. In an era of fierce corporate competition and stark party-line politics, this movement to improve STEM education and recruit more teachers stands as a pretty remarkable example of collaboration in the name of the greater good.

Huh. Imagine that. Teachers working toward the greater good. Go figure.

Ways to help this awesome movement be even awesomer

For one, have you ever considered teaching STEM?

Or maybe you already are a teacher? Or maybe you like your job, but believe in this mission? Or maybe you have a weird about-page fetish, and now you wanna take this to the next level? Whatever it may be, the least you can do is share the living crap out of this site, our animation, and anything else you find interesting, funny, or worthy of your precious social media channels.

But maybe the best thing you could do is think really hard who you know, and who among them might make for a killer high school computer science teacher or grade school math teacher. Maybe your niece has a physics degree but no job, maybe your neighbor wants a career change, or maybe the person reading over your shoulder right now just really loves sharing and thus would make for a great teacher.

Point is, these subjects are awesome. They’re mind blowing. And the opportunity to spread that knowledge is one of the most incredible opportunities afforded to not just our nation, but our species.
